# Child and Family Services

Chapter 7 of the ISC BC Region 2024-2025 Program Guide covers funding to support the safety and well-being of First Nations children ordinarily resident on reserve, delivered through culturally appropriate prevention and protection services. It reflects the ongoing reform of the First Nations Child and Family Services (FNCFS) program following the 2016 Canadian Human Rights Tribunal (CHRT) ruling, the December 2021 Agreement-in-Principle on Long-Term Reform, and the implementation of *An Act respecting First Nations, Inuit and Métis children, youth and families* (in force January 1, 2020). The main funding streams are Indigenous Child and Family Services Agencies, Jordan's Principle, capital funding (CHRT 41), and Jurisdiction & Engagement.

## General Information & What's New

In January 2016 the CHRT found the FNCFS program to be flawed, inequitable, and discriminatory under the *Canadian Human Rights Act*, ordering ISC to cease discriminatory practices and reform the program. ISC is moving to a new evidence-informed funding approach providing stable, predictable, non-discriminatory funding to promote family wellness and reduce the over-representation of First Nations children in care.

*An Act respecting First Nations, Inuit and Métis children, youth and families* (the "Act") establishes national standards (best interests of the child, cultural continuity, substantive equality) and affirms the inherent right to self-government, including jurisdiction over child and family services. The three key federal implementation areas are: capacity-building; coordination agreement discussions; and implementation of Indigenous child and family service models.

## Agreement-in-Principle (Long-Term Reform)

On December 31, 2021, the parties to the CHRT complaint (FNCFS Program and Jordan's Principle) reached an Agreement-in-Principle for Long-Term Reform — a framework to reform FNCFS, improve Jordan's Principle implementation, and prevent recurrence of discrimination.

**What it funds** — As of April 1, 2022, Canada began funding:
- **Prevention** — formula of \$2,500 × the on-reserve First Nations population and First Nations population in the Yukon (allocated among agencies and First Nations; adjusted annually for inflation and population changes).
- **First Nations Representative Services** — formula of \$283 × the First Nations population on-reserve and in the Yukon.
- **Post-majority care** — actual costs of post-majority care to former children in care up to and including age 27 in BC.

All other funding areas remain unchanged, including needs-based operations funding and the claims process for actuals (protection, intake/investigations, legal services, building repairs).

**Funding methodology** — Based on CHRT Orders. First Nations affirming jurisdiction under the Act will not receive less funding than under the reformed approach; they are funded through grant mechanisms under the Act.

**Key contacts** — Funding formula/admin procedures: bccfs@sac-isc.gc.ca. Jurisdiction Initiative funding options: bccfsjurisdiction-juridiction@sac-isc.gc.ca.

## 2022 CHRT 41 — Capital Funding

**Purpose / what it funds** — Per CHRT 41 (consent order issued January 18, 2022, amended), First Nations, FNCFS agencies, and First Nations-authorized Jordan's Principle service providers can access funding for the planning, purchase, and construction of capital assets on-reserve (or off-reserve where no suitable/accessible on-reserve property exists) to:
- support delivery of funded FNCFS programming to children and families on-reserve and in Yukon; and
- provide safe, accessible, confidential, culturally- and age-appropriate spaces to deliver Jordan's Principle funded services on-reserve, including space to administer them.

Based on actual costs incurred on or after August 26, 2021.

**Eligibility** — See program Terms and Conditions (https://www.sac-isc.gc.ca/eng/1648577221890/1648577242550). Note: ICFSA may continue to seek reimbursement for building repairs through the business plan approach until a reformed FNCFS program is implemented.

**How to apply** — Use the Capital Funding Request Form (see "Funding for capital assets" website).

**Key contacts** — BC Region CHRT 41 inquiries: bccfs@sac-isc.gc.ca (also listed as bcjpchrt41-cbpjtcdp41@sac-isc.gc.ca). Submit requests: chrt41-tcdp41@sac-isc.gc.ca.

## Indigenous Child and Family Services Agencies (ICFSA)

**Purpose / who's eligible** — The FNCFS program funds culturally appropriate prevention and protection services for First Nations children ordinarily resident on reserve and registered (or entitled to be registered) under the *Indian Act*. First Nations and ICFSA may share prevention resources provided allocations promote family wellness, prevent child maltreatment, and fulfill statutory child welfare requirements.

**What it funds (cost considerations for Operations and Care & Maintenance)** — Actual costs in the best interest of the child; costs normal/necessary to the activity; costs meeting accounting/legal requirements; the distinct cultural, historical, and geographic needs of each child/family (substantive equality); and costs for children ordinarily resident on reserve.

### Maintenance Expenditures

**What it funds** — Costs of maintaining a child placed out of the parental home per provincial standards, submitted monthly by both MCFD and ICFSA. Includes non-medical services for children with behavioral/specialized needs; provincially-approved purchases not otherwise covered; monthly out-of-home placement costs (foster care, group homes, institutional care, kinship care); post-adoption subsidies/supports; and professional services not covered by other jurisdictions or the Non-Insured Health Benefits Program. Reimbursed on actual costs.

**How to apply** — Submit the annual Business Plan plus: (1) copy of approved delegation confirmation agreement (bi-lateral agency/province agreement); (2) certificate of good standing; (3) list of directors with specimen signatures; and (4-5) board resolution(s) identifying the person authorized to sign funding agreements/amendments (if designating an individual; may be valid for more than one fiscal year). Required to initiate a new-year funding agreement.

**What to report** — Maintenance reimbursed per **DCI #455917** "Child and Family Services Maintenance Report" (due last day of each month, ICFSA only if applicable). Additional maintenance forms:
- **Notice of Admission (NOA)** — PAW #493710 (2024-2025): submitted by ICFSA so ISC can determine financial responsibility for a child being added to the monthly maintenance report.
- **Notice of Discharge (NOD)** — PAW #493738 (2024-2025): submitted by ICFSA to notify that a child has been discharged/transferred out and is no longer in the Agency's care.

Other FNCFS reporting (from the Calendar of Due Dates):

## Jordan's Principle

**Purpose** — Named in memory of Jordan River Anderson (Norway House Cree Nation), Jordan's Principle ensures all First Nations children in Canada can access the products, services, and supports they need, when they need them, across health, social, and educational needs — including the distinct needs of Two-Spirit/LGBTQQIA children and youth and those with disabilities. Since 2016 the Government has committed \$3.5 billion. Following CHRT rulings, requests apply substantive equality, culturally appropriate services, and the best interests of the child via a non-discriminatory "Back-to-Basics" approach.

**Who's covered (CHRT ruling Nov 25, 2020)** — A child under the age of majority in their province/territory, permanently resident in Canada, who meets one of: registered/eligible under the *Indian Act*; has a parent/guardian registered/eligible under the *Indian Act*; is recognized by their Nation for Jordan's Principle purposes; or is ordinarily resident on reserve.

**Who can send requests** — Individual/family requests: a parent/guardian of an on-reserve First Nations/Indigenous child; a First Nations/Indigenous child on reserve aged over 16; a child at the age of consent; or an authorized representative (with parent/guardian consent). Group requests (multiple families): a parent/guardian; a community or Tribal Council; a community organization/institution; or a service coordinator, navigator, or case manager.

**What it funds (examples, case-by-case, no fixed list)** — Health: vehicle modification, transportation to appointments, speech/physio/occupational therapy, mental health services, medical supplies & equipment, assessments/screenings, wheelchair ramps, mobility aids. Social: personal support worker, specialized summer camps, respite care, culturally-based programs. Education: school supplies, tutoring, assistive technologies, psycho-educational assessments, teaching assistants, specialized school transportation. Capital assets are also available under CHRT 41 (reimbursement for building repairs may continue via actuals per 2018 CHRT 4).

**How to apply** — Contact the National Call Centre (24/7) or your BC regional representative to start a request — **PAW: ongoing intake**. BC Region also runs an Enhanced Service Coordination Model with 45+ Community-Based and Regional Service Coordinators.

**What to report** — Due July 29 (or as indicated in funding agreement):
- **DCI #HC-P111** — Jordan's Principle – Service Delivery, Annual Report.
- **DCI #HC-P113** — Jordan's Principle – Service Coordination, Annual Report.
- **DCI #F-0190** — Annual Audited Financial Statement.

## Child and Family Services Reform — Jurisdiction & Engagement

The Act affirms the inherent right to self-government including jurisdiction over child and family services. The federal implementation approach has three key areas:

- **Capacity-Building** — Funding for Indigenous governing bodies and section 35 rights-holding communities planning to exercise jurisdiction; supports codification of Indigenous laws, development of service models, and community consultation.
- **Coordination Agreement Discussions** — Tripartite forums to discuss coordinating measures supporting an Indigenous governing body's exercise of jurisdiction.
- **Implementation of Indigenous Child and Family Services Models** — ISC supports implementation through sustainable, needs-based funding consistent with substantive equality.

**How to apply** — PAW: **N/A**. Funding application package to include: Work Plan (vision, expected outcomes, objectives, activities); Budget (estimated costs per activity); information on deferred revenue if applicable (surplus from prior-year capacity-building funding and plans to use it); and Indigenous governing body authorization (e.g., Band Council Resolution or letter authorizing the entity per the Act, signed by each section 35 rights-holding nation/community if representing multiple). Continuous intake — submission strongly recommended by **December 6, 2024** for timely processing within FY 2024-25. Inquiries: bccfsjurisdiction-juridiction@sac-isc.gc.ca.

**What to report** — Both due **April 30, 2025** under **DCI #4548549**:
- Activities and Expenditures Report – Jurisdiction Capacity Development (Capacity-building).
- Activities and Expenditures Report – Coordination Agreement Discussion (CAD) Table.

**Funding methodology** — Comparable across all groups/distinctions, based on each Indigenous community's/governing body's distinct circumstances (population, remoteness, and number of section 35 rights-holding communities represented).

**Key contacts** — BC Jurisdiction & Engagement: bccfsjurisdiction-juridiction@sac-isc.gc.ca.
